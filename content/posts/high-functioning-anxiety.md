                <ul class="space-y-3 list-disc list-inside text-gray-800">
                    <li><strong>Not a Formal Diagnosis:</strong> High-functioning anxiety describes a pattern where individuals experience significant internal anxiety while maintaining high levels of outward performance and success.</li>
                    <li><strong>Hidden Struggle:</strong> The external success often masks internal distress, including overthinking, perfectionism, fear of failure, and physical symptoms like fatigue or muscle tension.</li>
                    <li><strong>Subtle Signs:</strong> Look for over-commitment, difficulty relaxing, constant busyness, needing reassurance, and harsh self-criticism despite achievements.</li>
                    <li><strong>Potential Costs:</strong> Unaddressed high-functioning anxiety can lead to burnout, relationship strain, decreased joy, and potentially escalate to more severe anxiety or depression.</li>
                    <li><strong>Management is Possible:</strong> Strategies include therapy (like CBT), setting boundaries, practicing self-compassion, mindfulness, lifestyle adjustments, and seeking professional support even if you seem "fine".</li>
                </ul>

            <!-- Introduction -->
            <section id="introduction-main">
                <h2 class="text-3xl font-bold">Introduction: The Hidden Pressure</h2>
                 <p class="text-lg leading-relaxed mb-6">You know the person (or maybe it’s you) — the one who seems to have it all together: the high performer, the one bouncing from project to project, always on-time, always polished. On the outside they look calm, competent and in control. But inside? There’s a different story: constant “what ifs”, second-guessing, that feeling of never being quite enough. That interior tension is what we mean when we talk about <strong>high-functioning anxiety</strong>.</p>
                 <p class="text-lg leading-relaxed mb-6">Although it’s not a clinical diagnosis in the official sense (like Generalized Anxiety Disorder or Panic Disorder found in the DSM-5), it’s a very real experience—and one that deserves attention.</p>
                 
             </section>

             <!-- What is High-Functioning Anxiety? -->
            <section id="what-is-hfa">
                 <h2 class="text-3xl font-bold">What is High-Functioning Anxiety?</h2>
                 <p class="text-lg leading-relaxed mb-6">“High-functioning anxiety” describes individuals who experience many common anxiety symptoms—worry, overthinking, physical tension—but continue to manage daily responsibilities, achieve success, and maintain an outward appearance of "doing fine." <span class="citation">(Verywell Mind, Cleveland Clinic)</span></p>
                 <p class="text-lg leading-relaxed mb-6">According to the Mayo Clinic Health System, it can be seen as a pattern where the person doesn't necessarily avoid anxiety-provoking situations but instead pushes through them, often at significant internal cost. <span class="citation">(Mayo Clinic Health System)</span></p>
                <div class="info-box">
                    <p class="text-center font-semibold text-lg">In short: Outward Success + Inner Struggle.</p>
                </div>
            </section>

            <!-- Signs & Symptoms -->
            <section id="signs-symptoms">
                <h2 class="text-3xl font-bold">Signs & Symptoms to Watch For</h2>
                <p class="text-lg leading-relaxed mb-6">Because high-functioning anxiety doesn't always look like classic panic or avoidance, its signs can be subtle or masked. Key indicators include:</p>
                <ul>
                    <li><strong>Constant Busyness & High Achievement:</strong> Always productive, delivering results, but often driven by an undercurrent of fear, worry, or self-criticism. <span class="citation">(Cleveland Clinic)</span></li>
                    <li><strong>Perfectionism:</strong> Setting incredibly high standards for oneself and being overly harsh about perceived failures or mistakes. <span class="citation">(Montare Behavioral Health)</span></li>
                    <li><strong>Overthinking & Rumination:</strong> Constantly replaying conversations, second-guessing decisions, worrying about future 'what-ifs', and imagining worst-case outcomes. <span class="citation">(SummitStone Health Partners)</span></li>
                    <li><strong>Difficulty Saying "No":</strong> Over-committing and taking on too much, often stemming from a fear of disappointing others or appearing incapable. <span class="citation">(Cleveland Clinic)</span></li>
                    <li><strong>Hidden Physical Symptoms:</strong> Experiencing physical signs of anxiety (muscle tension, stomach issues, headaches, fatigue, racing heart, poor sleep) despite appearing calm externally. <span class="citation">(Mayo Clinic Health System)</span></li>
                    <li><strong>Internal Fears:</strong> Persistent feelings of inadequacy, imposter syndrome (fear of being "found out"), or worry about eventually failing to keep up the pace. <span class="citation">(Mayo Clinic News Network)</span></li>
                    <li><strong>The Performance Paradox:</strong> Maintaining a capable, composed exterior specifically *because* of the internal anxiety—using achievement as a coping mechanism.</li>
                </ul>
            </section>

            <!-- How It Shows Up -->
             <section id="how-it-shows-up">
                 <h2 class="text-3xl font-bold">How It Shows Up in Life</h2>
                 
                 <h3>At Work / Career</h3>
                 <p class="text-lg leading-relaxed mb-6">You might be the go-to person, highly reliable, and rarely miss a deadline. You might volunteer for extra tasks to prove your worth or avoid letting others down. However, this often comes with staying late, excessive worry about mistakes, difficulty disconnecting after work, and feeling restless or unable to relax. Your sense of self-worth may become tightly linked to your productivity, making it hard to slow down without feeling anxious or guilty.</p>
                

                 <h3>In Relationships & Social Life</h3>
                 <p class="text-lg leading-relaxed mb-6">Socially, you may appear engaged, outgoing, and capable. Internally, however, your mind might be racing, analyzing interactions, or worrying about judgment. You might avoid showing vulnerability, feeling pressure to maintain the "strong" or "put-together" image. Saying yes to social engagements when you feel drained, out of fear of missing out or disappointing friends, is common.</p>

                 <h3>Internally & In the Body</h3>
                 <p class="text-lg leading-relaxed mb-6">Even when everything looks fine on the surface, there can be a constant internal loop of worry, planning, and self-critique. Physically, this might manifest as feeling perpetually "on edge," restless, or experiencing a state of being "tired but wired." Sleep disruptions are frequent. Physical symptoms like headaches, stomach problems, or muscle aches might be dismissed as "just stress" but represent the body's response to chronic anxiety.</p>
             </section>

            <!-- The Hidden Cost -->
            <section id="hidden-cost">
                <h2 class="text-3xl font-bold">The Hidden Cost</h2>
                <p class="text-lg leading-relaxed mb-6">Because the anxiety is masked by functioning, it often goes unaddressed, but this doesn't mean it's harmless. The constant internal pressure, relentless perfectionism, and suppression of needs for rest and self-care can take a significant toll.</p>
                <div class="info-box">
                    <p><strong>Potential long-term consequences include:</strong></p>
                    <ul>
                        <li><strong>Burnout:</strong> Complete physical and emotional exhaustion.</li>
                        <li><strong>Increased Risk of Other Conditions:</strong> Potential escalation into diagnosable anxiety disorders, depression, or physical health problems related to chronic stress.</li>
                        <li><strong>Relationship Strain:</strong> Difficulty with vulnerability and connection due to maintaining a facade.</li>
                        <li><strong>Reduced Joy & Fulfillment:</strong> Living in a constant state of striving and worry can prevent enjoyment of successes and simple pleasures.</li>
                    </ul>
                </div>
            </section>

            <!-- What Contributes -->
            <section id="contributors">
                 <h2 class="text-3xl font-bold">What Contributes to It?</h2>
                 <p class="text-lg leading-relaxed mb-6">High-functioning anxiety typically stems from a combination of factors:</p>
                 <ul>
                    <li><strong>Personality Traits:</strong> Often seen in individuals who are naturally conscientious, detail-oriented, high-achieving, or sensitive to criticism and perceived failure.</li>
                    <li><strong>Early Experiences:</strong> Growing up in environments with high expectations, where performance was heavily emphasized, or where expressing vulnerability was discouraged. <span class="citation">(Mayo Clinic Health System)</span></li>
                    <li><strong>Cultural & Societal Pressures:</strong> The demands of modern fast-paced life, comparison culture fueled by social media, and "always-on" work expectations.</li>
                    <li><strong>Biological Predisposition:</strong> A potential family history of anxiety disorders or a naturally higher sensitivity to stress. <span class="citation">(Cleveland Clinic)</span></li>
                    <li><strong>Coping Style:</strong> Developing a pattern of pushing harder and achieving more as a way to manage or mask underlying anxiety, rather than withdrawing or avoiding.</li>
                </ul>
            </section>

            <!-- Practical Strategies -->
            <section id="strategies">
                 <h2 class="text-3xl font-bold">Practical Strategies: Managing & Thriving</h2>
                 <p class="text-lg leading-relaxed mb-6">Managing high-functioning anxiety involves acknowledging the internal struggle and implementing strategies to reduce pressure and increase well-being.</p>

                 <h3>Therapeutic / Professional Support</h3>
                 <ul>
                    <li><strong>Consider Therapy:</strong> Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) is effective for identifying and challenging anxious thought patterns and perfectionistic behaviors. <span class="citation">(Cleveland Clinic)</span> Other modalities like ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy) can also be helpful.</li>
                    <li><strong>Medication:</strong> If symptoms are significantly impacting quality of life, medication might be discussed with a healthcare provider. <span class="citation">(Cleveland Clinic)</span></li>
                    <li><strong>Professional Assessment:</strong> Don't dismiss your struggles just because you're functioning. Seeking a professional opinion can provide validation and tailored guidance. <span class="citation">(Mayo Clinic News Network)</span></li>
                </ul>

                <h3>Lifestyle & Self-Care Strategies</h3>
                 <ul>
                    <li><strong>Internal Check-ins:</strong> Regularly pause to notice physical sensations (tension, breathing) and thoughts without judgment.</li>
                    <li><strong>Mindfulness & Presence:</strong> Practice techniques to ground yourself in the present moment, reducing rumination about past or future. <span class="citation">(PositivePsychology.com)</span></li>
                    <li><strong>Boundary Setting:</strong> Learn to say "no" to non-essential commitments. Delegate tasks when possible. Protect your time for rest and recovery.</li>
                    <li><strong>Challenge Perfectionism:</strong> Intentionally practice "good enough." Set realistic standards and practice self-compassion when you fall short.</li>
                    <li><strong>Prioritize Physical Health:</strong> Ensure adequate sleep, engage in regular movement you enjoy, and maintain balanced nutrition to build resilience.</li>
                    <li><strong>Reframe Productivity:</strong> Recognize that rest, connection, and simply "being" are as valuable as "doing." Your worth is inherent, not solely based on output.</li>
                    <li><strong>Build a Support Network:</strong> Confide in trusted friends or family. Sharing your experience can reduce isolation and provide perspective.</li>
                </ul>

                <!-- 7-Step Toolkit -->
                <div id="toolkit" class="info-box mt-8">
                     <h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-4 text-primary !border-none !pl-0"><i class="fas fa-toolbox mr-2"></i>Your 7-Step Toolkit for Managing HFA</h3>
                     <ol class="list-decimal !ml-6 space-y-3">
                         <li><strong>Internal Check:</strong> Ask yourself daily: “How am I *really* feeling right now, beneath the surface?”</li>
                         <li><strong>Thought Log:</strong> Spend 5 minutes noting down perfectionist, self-critical, or worst-case scenario thoughts. Just observe them.</li>
                         <li><strong>Schedule Recovery:</strong> Block out non-negotiable time for rest or enjoyable, non-productive activities in your calendar.</li>
                         <li><strong>Boundary Audit:</strong> Identify one area where you feel overloaded. Can you say no, delegate, or reduce the scope?</li>
                         <li><strong>'Done List' Journal:</strong> At the end of the day, list what you *did* accomplish, rather than focusing only on the to-do list. Practice self-acknowledgment.</li>
                         <li><strong>Movement/Breath Pause:</strong> Incorporate a 5-10 minute daily break for stretching, walking, or simple deep breathing exercises.</li>
                         <li><strong>Self-Compassion Check-in:</strong> Ask: “If a friend I cared about was feeling this pressure, what gentle advice or support would I offer them?” Try offering that to yourself.</li>
                     </ol>
                 </div>
