            <section id="how-they-work" class="bg-white p-8 rounded-lg shadow-md">
                <h2 class="!mt-0">How Do Countertop Reverse Osmosis Systems Work?</h2>
                
                <p class="mb-6 text-gray-700">Understanding how a <strong>countertop reverse osmosis water filter system</strong> works helps you appreciate why these systems deliver such superior water quality compared to basic filter pitchers or faucet filters.</p>

                <h3 class="!mt-6">The Basics of Reverse Osmosis Filtration</h3>
                <p class="mb-4 text-gray-700"><strong>Reverse osmosis</strong> is a water purification process that uses pressure to force water molecules through a semipermeable membrane. This membrane acts like an ultra-fine barrier with microscopic pores (typically 0.0001 microns)—small enough to allow only water molecules to pass through while blocking contaminants.</p>

                <div class="bg-blue-50 p-6 rounded-lg my-6">
                    <h4 class="font-semibold text-lg mb-3">What Size Are Contaminants?</h4>
                    <ul class="space-y-2 text-sm text-gray-700">
                        <li><strong>Water Molecule:</strong> 0.0001 microns (passes through)</li>
                        <li><strong>RO Membrane Pore Size:</strong> 0.0001 microns</li>
                        <li><strong>Dissolved Salts:</strong> 0.0003-0.0006 microns (BLOCKED)</li>
                        <li><strong>Bacteria:</strong> 0.2-2 microns (BLOCKED)</li>
                        <li><strong>Viruses:</strong> 0.02-0.3 microns (BLOCKED)</li>
                        <li><strong>Parasites:</strong> 2-50 microns (BLOCKED)</li>
                    </ul>
                </div>

                <h3>The Complete Filtration Process: Stage by Stage</h3>
                <p class="mb-4 text-gray-700">A typical <strong>countertop RO system</strong> uses 4-6 filtration stages:</p>

                <div class="space-y-6 mb-8">
                    <div class="border-l-4 border-primary pl-4">
                        <h4 class="font-semibold text-lg mb-2">Stage 1: Pre-Filtration (Sediment Removal)</h4>
                        <p class="text-gray-700">The first filter removes larger particles like sediment, rust, sand, and debris. This protects the more delicate RO membrane from damage and clogging. Typically rated at 5 microns, this filter catches anything you can see with the naked eye.</p>
                    </div>

                    <div class="border-l-4 border-primary pl-4">
                        <h4 class="font-semibold text-lg mb-2">Stage 2: Carbon Pre-Filtration</h4>
                        <p class="text-gray-700">Activated carbon filter removes chlorine, volatile organic compounds (VOCs), pesticides, and substances that cause bad tastes and odors. This stage is crucial because chlorine can damage the RO membrane.</p>
                    </div>

                    <div class="border-l-4 border-accent pl-4">
                        <h4 class="font-semibold text-lg mb-2">Stage 3: Reverse Osmosis Membrane (The Main Event)</h4>
                        <p class="text-gray-700 mb-2">This is where the magic happens. The RO membrane removes:</p>
                        <ul class="list-disc list-inside space-y-1 text-sm text-gray-700 ml-4">
                            <li>95-99% of total dissolved solids (TDS)</li>
                            <li>Heavy metals: lead, mercury, arsenic, chromium</li>
                            <li>Fluoride and chlorine residue</li>

                        <p class="text-gray-700">Some systems add beneficial minerals back into the water (calcium, magnesium) for improved taste and pH balance.</p>
                    </div>
                </div>

                <h3>Why Countertop Models Work Differently Than Under-Sink</h3>
                <p class="mb-4 text-gray-700">Traditional under-sink RO systems use water line pressure (40-60 PSI) to push water through the membrane. <strong>Countertop reverse osmosis systems</strong> typically use one of two methods:</p>

                <ol class="list-decimal list-inside space-y-3 mb-6 text-gray-700">
                    <li><strong>Built-in Pump Systems:</strong> Have an internal pump that creates the necessary pressure for RO filtration. These work independently of your water line pressure.</li>
                    <li><strong>Gravity-Fed Systems:</strong> Use elevated tanks and gravity to create pressure. These are quieter but slower.</li>
                </ol>

                <p class="text-gray-700">Both methods produce water quality identical to under-sink systems—the only difference is how they generate the pressure needed for the RO process.</p>
