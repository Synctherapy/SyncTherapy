<section id="the-problem-oxidative-stress">
    <h2 class="text-3xl font-bold mb-6">The Problem: Exercise-Induced Oxidative Stress</h2>
    <p class="mb-4 text-gray-700">When you exercise intensely—whether that is lifting heavy weights, doing CrossFit, or running a 10K—your body demands massive amounts of oxygen to create cellular energy (ATP).</p>
    <p class="mb-4 text-gray-700">However, this massive increase in cellular respiration comes with a toxic byproduct: <strong>Reactive Oxygen Species (ROS)</strong>, commonly known as free radicals.</p>
    
    <div class="bg-white p-6 rounded-lg shadow-sm border border-gray-100 mb-6">
        <p class="text-gray-700 italic">Think of your muscles like a car engine. As you burn fuel (carbs and fats) to go faster, the engine creates exhaust. In your body, that "exhaust" is oxidative stress.</p>
    </div>
    
    <p class="mb-4 text-gray-700">While a small amount of oxidative stress is actually necessary to trigger muscle growth, severe exercise induces massive amounts of toxic free radicals (specifically the highly destructive hydroxyl radical). These radicals cause immense cellular damage, leading to massive inflammation, muscle fatigue, and Delayed Onset Muscle Soreness (DOMS).</p>
</section>

<section id="how-hydrogen-solves-this">

    <h3 class="text-2xl font-semibold mt-8 mb-4">1. It Scavenges the Worst Free Radicals</h3>
    <p class="mb-4 text-gray-700">Unlike traditional antioxidants (like Vitamin C or E) which will blindly neutralize <em>all</em> free radicals (even the good ones you need for muscle adaptation), H₂ is called a <strong>selective antioxidant</strong>. </p>
    <p class="mb-4 text-gray-700">Because it is the smallest molecule in the universe, it easily passes through cell membranes and directly into the mitochondria. Once inside, it specifically seeks out and neutralizes only the highly toxic hydroxyl radicals, turning them harmlessly into water (H₂O). </p>
    <p class="mb-4 text-gray-800 font-semibold bg-gray-100 p-3 rounded">The Result: Massive reduction in cellular damage and inflammation, drastically limiting muscle soreness the next day.</p>
    
    <h3 class="text-2xl font-semibold mt-8 mb-4">2. It Reduces Lactic Acid Accumulation</h3>
    <p class="mb-4 text-gray-700">During high-intensity sprints or heavy lifts, your muscles rely on anaerobic glycolysis, leading to lactic acid build-up. This causes the famous "burn" and forces your muscles to fail.</p>

Hydrogen helps buffer the acidic environment, allowing athletes to push harder, for longer, before encountering muscle fatigue.</p>
