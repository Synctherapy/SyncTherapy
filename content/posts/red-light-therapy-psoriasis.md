            <div class="bg-white p-6 rounded-lg shadow-md">

Unlike traditional <strong>phototherapy for psoriasis</strong> that uses UV light, red light therapy works without the risks of skin damage or increased cancer risk. Research has shown that <a href="https://synctherapylocal.local/red-light-therapy-boosts-mitochondrial-function/">red light therapy benefits</a> include reducing inflammation, promoting wound healing, and alleviating pain. Additionally, it has been found to enhance collagen production, leading to improved skin texture and elasticity. As a non-invasive treatment option, it has gained popularity among those seeking alternatives to more aggressive therapies.</p>

                <h3 class="text-xl font-bold mb-4">Key Clinical Findings</h3>

                        <p class="text-gray-700 mb-2">Nine patients with chronic, treatment-resistant psoriasis received combination 830nm (near-infrared) and 633nm (red) LED therapy. <strong>Results: 60-100% clearance rates</strong> after just 4-5 weeks of twice-weekly sessions.</p>
                        <p class="text-sm text-gray-500"><a href="https://doi.org/10.1089/pho.2009.2484" target="_blank" rel="noopener" class="text-primary hover:underline">DOI: 10.1089/pho.2009.2484</a></p>

                    <div class="border-l-4 border-green-500 pl-4 bg-green-50 p-4 rounded-r-lg">

                        <p class="text-gray-700 mb-2">Comprehensive review of phototherapy for psoriasis. Found that <strong>near-infrared and visible red light show promise</strong> for treating psoriasis due to strong tissue penetration and encouraging photobiomodulation effects—without systemic adverse events.</p>
                        <p class="text-sm text-gray-500"><a href="https://doi.org/10.1007/s10103-017-2360-1" target="_blank" rel="noopener" class="text-primary hover:underline">DOI: 10.1007/s10103-017-2360-1</a></p>

                        <p class="text-gray-700 mb-2">Pilot study testing red/NIR-emitting fabric on psoriasis patients. Three patients who completed the 12-week study noted improvement, with <strong>measurable reductions in lesion redness, thickness, and scale</strong>.</p>
                        <p class="text-sm text-gray-500"><a href="https://doi.org/10.1080/09546634.2023.2251619" target="_blank" rel="noopener" class="text-primary hover:underline">DOI: 10.1080/09546634.2023.2251619</a></p>

                        <p class="text-gray-700 mb-2">Tested whether red light causes DNA damage in human skin cells. <strong>Result: No measurable DNA damage</strong> even at high doses (up to 1280 J/cm²), supporting the safety of red light therapy for skin treatment.</p>
                        <p class="text-sm text-gray-500"><a href="https://doi.org/10.1002/jbio.202200023" target="_blank" rel="noopener" class="text-primary hover:underline">DOI: 10.1002/jbio.202200023</a></p>
