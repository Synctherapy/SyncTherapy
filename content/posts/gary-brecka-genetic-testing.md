<h3>Basics of Genetic Tests</h3>
<p>Genetic tests look at a person's DNA to find changes or variations. These tests can show if someone has a higher chance of getting certain diseases. They also help doctors choose the best treatments.</p>
<p>Genetic testing often uses a blood or saliva sample. Labs examine the sample to find specific gene changes. Results can take a few weeks to come back.</p>
<p>Some common reasons for genetic testing include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Finding inherited diseases</li>
<li>Checking for cancer risks</li>
<li>Guiding medication choices</li>
<li>Planning for pregnancy</li>
</ul>
<h3>The Role of Biomarkers in Health Outcomes</h3>
<p>Biomarkers are measurable signs in the body. They can be genes, proteins, or other molecules. Doctors use biomarkers to check health and predict disease risks.</p>
<p>Genetic testing and biomarkers work together to give a full picture of health. For example, certain gene changes might raise the levels of a protein biomarker. This could mean a higher risk for a specific disease.</p>
<p>Biomarkers help track:</p>
<ul>
<li>Disease progress</li>
<li>Treatment effectiveness</li>
<li>Drug responses</li>
</ul>

<h3>Ethical Use of Genetic Information</h3>
<p>The ethical use of genetic data is crucial. Key considerations include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Informed consent: People must understand and agree to how their data will be used.</li>
<li>Non-discrimination: Genetic information should not be used to discriminate in employment or insurance.</li>
<li>Data ownership: Who owns genetic data - the individual or the company?</li>
<li>Unexpected findings: How to handle discoveries of serious health risks.</li>
</ul>
<p>Some countries have laws to protect genetic privacy. The U.S. has the Genetic Information Nondiscrimination Act (GINA).</p>
<p>Companies should be transparent about how they use genetic data. This builds trust with customers. Ethical guidelines help ensure genetic information is used responsibly.</p>
