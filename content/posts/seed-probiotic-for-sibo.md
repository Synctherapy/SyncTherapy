            <h2 class="text-3xl font-black text-gray-900 mb-6">The Controversy: Probiotics and SIBO</h2>
            <div class="mb-6">
                <p class="mb-4">The use of probiotics for SIBO remains controversial for several important reasons:</p>
                <ul class="space-y-3">
                    <li class="flex items-start"><i class="fas fa-exclamation-circle text-red-500 mt-1 mr-3"></i> <span><strong>Bacterial Load Concern:</strong> Traditional probiotics can add millions of bacteria to an already overpopulated small intestine, potentially worsening symptoms.</span></li>
                    <li class="flex items-start"><i class="fas fa-exclamation-circle text-red-500 mt-1 mr-3"></i> <span><strong>D-Lactate Production:</strong> Certain Lactobacillus strains produce D-lactate, which may contribute to brain fog, fatigue, and cognitive symptoms in SIBO patients.</span></li>
                    <li class="flex items-start"><i class="fas fa-exclamation-circle text-red-500 mt-1 mr-3"></i> <span><strong>Fermentable Prebiotics:</strong> Most probiotic supplements contain FOS, inulin, or other prebiotic fibers that feed overgrown bacteria, creating more gas and bloating.</span></li>
                </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-primary p-4 rounded-lg">
                <p class="font-semibold text-gray-900">My Clinical Approach:</p>
                <p class="text-gray-700">In my practice, I don't recommend any probiotic—including Seed—during active SIBO treatment (typically 4-6 weeks of herbal antimicrobials like Candibactin-AR/BR). However, once treatment is complete and symptoms have resolved, introducing a well-formulated probiotic like Seed can help prevent recurrence.</p>
            </div>
        </section>

            <h2 class="text-3xl font-black text-gray-900 mb-6">When to AVOID Seed During SIBO</h2>
            <div class="mb-6">
                <p class="mb-4">There are specific situations where Seed (or any probiotic) should be avoided during SIBO treatment:</p>
                <ul class="space-y-3">

                    <li class="flex items-start"><i class="fas fa-times-circle text-red-500 mt-1 mr-3"></i> <span><strong>D-Lactate Concerns:</strong> If you've experienced D-lactate acidosis symptoms (severe brain fog, confusion, fatigue), the multiple Lactobacillus strains in Seed may not be ideal during active treatment.</span></li>
                    <li class="flex items-start"><i class="fas fa-times-circle text-red-500 mt-1 mr-3"></i> <span><strong>Hydrogen Sulfide SIBO:</strong> This type of SIBO often requires a different approach, and probiotic supplementation should be guided by comprehensive testing.</span></li>
                    <li class="flex items-start"><i class="fas fa-times-circle text-red-500 mt-1 mr-3"></i> <span><strong>During Antimicrobial Protocol:</strong> While you're taking herbal antibiotics or prescription rifaximin, focus on treatment rather than recolonization.</span></li>

                <div class="bg-white p-5 rounded-xl border border-gray-200">
                    <h3 class="font-bold text-lg mb-2">Can I take Seed probiotic if I have SIBO?</h3>
                    <p class="text-gray-700">The answer depends on your SIBO status. If you have active SIBO with significant D-lactate concerns or hydrogen sulfide overgrowth, avoid Seed during treatment.                     <h3 class="font-bold text-lg mb-2">Why do some practitioners avoid probiotics during active SIBO?</h3>
                    <p class="text-gray-700">Traditional probiotics may add more bacterial load to an already overcrowded small intestine, potentially worsening symptoms. Additionally, certain probiotic strains can produce D-lactate, which may contribute to brain fog and fatigue in some SIBO patients.</p>
                </div>
                <div class="bg-white p-5 rounded-xl border border-gray-200">
                    <h3 class="font-bold text-lg mb-2">What makes Seed different for SIBO compared to other probiotics?</h3>
                    <p class="text-gray-700">Seed uses non-fermenting prebiotics (pomegranate punicalagins + pine bark polyphenols) instead of fermentable fibers like FOS or inulin. This reduces the risk of feeding overgrown bacteria. However, the 24-strain formula still adds bacteria, which requires careful timing in a SIBO protocol.</p>
                </div>
                <div class="bg-white p-5 rounded-xl border border-gray-200">
                    <h3 class="font-bold text-lg mb-2">When should I start taking Seed after SIBO treatment?</h3>
                    <p class="text-gray-700">Most practitioners recommend waiting 2-4 weeks after completing antimicrobial treatment before introducing any probiotic, including Seed. This allows your gut to stabilize and ensures the overgrowth has been fully cleared before adding new bacterial strains.</p>
                </div>

                    <h3 class="font-bold text-lg mb-2">Is Seed better than spore-based probiotics for SIBO?</h3>
                    <p class="text-gray-700">Spore-based probiotics (like Just Thrive or Megasporebiotic) are sometimes used during SIBO treatment because they don't colonize the small intestine. However, Seed offers more comprehensive microbiome support for post-treatment maintenance. The best choice depends on your specific situation and should be guided by a practitioner.</p>
